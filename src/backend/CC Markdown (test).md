## Foreword
Shanghai High School International Division (SHSID) is a large international school with over 3,000 students from around the world. Established in 1993, the school has been nurturing all of her students with care and love.

The High School Section pays critical attention to students' personalized growth, providing the foundations for their life-long development from four dimensions.

**The First Dimension: Character & Values.** We encourage respect, care, contribution, honesty, autonomy, integrity, confidence, friendship, responsibility, and resilience. We care about students' character cultivation, particularly their grit, zest, self-control, optimism, gratitude, and curiosity. By implementing the Homeroom System, Student Recognition System, a Peer Advisor Program, and Round-table Learning, SHSID has built an interactive learning community in which students can develop sound virtues and proper values.

**The Second Dimension: Knowledge & Skills.** SHSID identifies with the theory of 4C's, focusing on improving students' collaboration, communication, critical thinking, creativity as well as other skills essential in the 21st century. In both teaching and extracurricular activities, we emphasize the cultivation of students' metacognitive ability and apply the theory of multiple intelligences and ICT to our teaching and learning approaches. At SHSID, students have direct experience of different learning methods, such as problem-based, project-based, inquiry-based, experiential, and the traditional lecture-based learning methods. With the combination of formative and summative assessments, our assessment system is becoming increasingly comprehensive. By launching meticulously designed Global Citizen Program, Academy, Senior Project, Camping, and Assembly, we have created opportunities for students to share with each other their knowledge and skills.

**The Third Dimension: Practice & Innovation.** At SHSID, we apply knowledge to practice and pioneer new methods through said practice. Through a range of activities, students' hands-on ability, design thinking, and transdisciplinary thinking are developed, so too are their global awareness, team spirit, and leadership. In the arenas of ISEF, HiMCM, MUN, Debate, Decathlon, WSC, and CTB, students have fully expressed their spirit of youth and liveliness. ASB and Clubs have connected every student on campus. Programs such as Job Shadow, Subject Day, and STEM are great platforms for students to expand their potential.

**The Fourth Dimension: Artistry & Wellness.** We believe in the critical role that artistic culture plays in students' growth and spare no effort to support various kinds of artistic activities. We provide our students with ample opportunities to learn and strengthen their craftsmanship, musicianship, and performing arts. In their pursuit of arts, students develop their imagination, creation, aestheticism, and identity. There are diverse activities for students to exhibit their artistic talents, such as the Talent Show, Art Festival, Drama Festival, Summer Concert, Lunchtime Music, Fine Art Works Exhibition, and Photo Art Exhibition. We expect our students to maintain physical fitness and wellbeing, to carry forth good sportsmanship, and to keep in mind a sense of rule. SHSID sports team, the Stallions, has harnessed the athletic ability of the students. Sports Week is a celebration of this ability for all the teachers and students. Through the practice of First Aid and Fire Drills, SHSID has educated the students on the value of life and survival skills. Students' mental health is an equally important part of our work, and we have paid close attention to students' self-concept, demeanor, and temperament. We have carried out activities such as Parental Workshops, Parent School, Counseling, and Spirit Week to guard and promote students' mental health.

"It takes ten years to grow trees but a hundred years to rear people," as the Chinese saying goes. Pillared by the co-curriculum model and the curriculum map, the High School Section of SHSID is committed to serving the personalized and life-long growth of every student. We wish every SHSID student harvest joy and happiness in their growth!

— Ma, Feng High School Principal

## Course Enrollment Policy

### 1. Course Selection & Program Withdrawal
1. A new student is enrolled in the corresponding level of courses based on his/her performance in the entrance tests and interview.
2. Grade 10 students with certain qualifications can apply for IB Diploma (IBD), IB Certificate (IBC), and Advanced Placement (AP) courses or international AS & A-Level courses upon the receipt of application letters from their parents/guardians. The application must be approved by the school.
3. Before advancing to Grade 12, Grade 11 students who meet the requirements by the school can apply for some one-year AP courses upon the receipt of application letters from their parents/guardians. The application must be approved by the school.
4. Students at a higher level class who fail to meet the requirements and academic challenges, upon sufficient evidence found, will be forced to withdraw from the program and move to a more fitting level class.

### 2. Changing of courses and levels
1. When conditions for advancement are met, a student may apply for moving to a higher level of class. The parent or guardian of the student needs to submit a written application. Only after completing the Level-Advancement test (if necessary), and after his/her subject teacher, homeroom teacher, and Grade Supervisor have been consulted, may a student advance to a higher level (with the approval of the Director). The change of IB/AP levels should also approved by the IB/AP coordinator.
2. Students who don't meet the requirements or academic challenges of the advanced level, i.e. fail in exams of two or over two subjects within one semester, will be forced to move down to the lower level classes. However, generally students in advanced level classes are not allowed to move down to lower level classes. The school administration may, in its sole discretion, move a particular student to a lower level class if it determines, upon reviewing sufficient evidence, that such student's academic progression will be best served by the move. All students should seriously evaluate their choices of course levels and are encouraged to take more advanced level of classes offered by the school to better prepare themselves for the academic rigor of a university life.
3. If an IB/AP student can not finish the assignments or achieve the academic results as required by the school, he/she will be moved to a lower level class after being reported twice by the teacher.
4. Students are not allowed to change to a different class of the same level.
5. IB Diploma Program in SHSID has its independent class schedule based on IBDP course model structure, which can not fit in the school or AP course schedule. The application about the subjects transfer from IBDP to school or AP programs won't be supported because of the schedule confliction. Please make the serious course choice with a comprehensive understanding about personal integrated academic competence.

### 3. Policies for incoming freshman (9th graders) to know
When a student enters high school, the levels of elective courses will be determined by his or her performance in the previous school year, the level and test score in relevant courses and academic ethics. Besides, student's English proficiency will also be taken into consideration.

At the beginning of Grade 9, the students who were in Grade 8 Physics H level will continue to be placed into Grade 9 Physics H level. Some students in the Grade 8 Physics S level will be selected for the Grade 9 S+ level according to their test scores and classroom behavior. This is a holistic evaluation and placement process.

At the very beginning of Grade 9, there is just one level for Chemistry: N. The students will be placed into the S, S+, or H level through the chemistry placement test after the midterm test. The students level will be selected according to student's Chemistry Placement Test score midterm score, and the student's classroom behavior. This is a holistic evaluation and placement process.

*Note: Courses will not be available unless more than 5 students apply.*

## Grade 10-11 Course Selection Q&A
It's the time of the year for course selecting again, and students are facing difficult choices. The courses you choose now will have a definite impact on the rest of your high school life and much more beyond. To help students choose the courses that best fit their needs, we have invited several juniors from eleventh grade to give their opinions and recommendations on the course selection process. Read on to gain more insight while choosing courses that best fit your personal needs.

**Q1: If my English level is not very high, what courses should I consider when choosing from Modules Three and Four?**
A1: Modules Three and Four are more focused on professional skill and personal interest, so students' English level is not too important. Many courses offered, such as Business Management and Energy and the Environment, require a certain level of technical skill to do well in classes. Classes often do not assign a lot of homework; rather, your own skill level is the key determinant of your performance. For instance, the Chinese Geography course and other geography courses offered require the use of the GIS computer-based technology, which in turn call for a student's definite computer skill basics. Some courses offered, especially language courses, start with the course's basics and go through the course based on student interests.

**Q2: Should I choose six courses or seven courses?**
A2: The choice you make should be based on personal interests and the courses you choose. Six courses are recommended if you choose a course with a high amount of homework or a higher difficulty level. A smaller number of courses may be perfect for your needs and provide less pressure, especially if you have high-level courses like AP classes. With seven courses, a student can develop into a more well-rounded character and explore more fields. However, this does have some side effects: you must diverge your attention to more courses throughout your junior and senior years.

**Q3: Some sophomore students may believe that courses from Modules Three and Four are relatively easier. Is this true?**
A3: The answer to this question varies depending on the course chosen. This can be seen in some smaller language courses that students choose, which involve a high level of knowledge. Instead of choosing courses based on your future grades, you should choose courses based on your own interests and passions.

**Q4: How should I choose courses from Module One?**
A4: Module One is composed of Biology, Chemistry, and Physics. Students are expected to choose one to two courses in the three choices. We recommend students to choose courses wisely based on their future paths. Colleges across the world have different standards, and the courses you choose may differ due to this reason. If you plan on studying the Sciences in the future, it is recommended to choose Physics and/or Chemistry.

**Q5: How should I choose social study-type courses from Module Two?**
A5: As with other modules, courses from Module Two should be selected based on personal interest. Most Module Two courses require effort outside of class to supplement materials learned in class. Teachers will explain basic information, leaving the textbook materials and other supplementary information for the students to grasp. However, English levels must be considered when you choose these classes; textbooks for courses can be college-level and are often difficult to understand. The Western Civilization course requires a high level of English proficiency to do well in class. The US Government course, too, requires high English proficiency.

**Course Name: Organic Chemistry**
- Course Code: `CHE012E110`
- Grade: 12
- Duration: 1 Year
- Prerequisites: G11 AP Chemistry or equivalent.
- Course Content and Aim: This course is a fundamental course in organic chemistry based upon the modern concepts of structure and mechanism of reactions. Topics of study mainly include: 1) bonding and structures of organic molecules; 2) the concepts of nomenclature, isomerism, and reaction mechanism; 3) the relationship between structure and reactivity of organic functional groups; 4) study of important types of reactions, with emphasis on synthetic usefulness and mechanisms; 5) the theoretical basis and applications of modern spectroscopic methods such as Infrared, mass spectroscopy and nuclear magnetic resonance spectroscopy; 6) exploring biologically important organic molecules such as carbohydrates, proteins, lipids, and nucleic acids. Modern chromatographic, instrumental, and spectroscopic techniques are applied to these investigations and experiments.

## Project-based Learning
The project-based learning course comes in two levels: Standard level and Challenge level. In standard level, students complete the entire investigation under the guidance of teacher, who sets the guiding questions and sets the direction of investigation for students. Challenge level does not involve a set research question. Instead, students are expected to investigate and propose research questions of their own, according to their interests and abilities, which will guide them through the entire problem-solving process.

## R-TEW
R-TEW is a weekly course designed to help students develop their reading, thinking, writing, and empathetic tools. In addition, the class will focus on the development of soft skills, such as discussion and using language in a given context for a specific purpose. Each week, students will be given a selected topic. Selected students will be tasked with posting articles related to the topic on blackboard. The remainder of the class will then digitally comment on the articles. After the black board posts have been reviewed by the entire class, the students will be meet for a weekly, seminar style, discussion of the week's theme.
